Electrical Subsystem


The electrical system comprises of two parts: the real time control system and the user interface system. The real time control system is based on an Arduino Uno V3. The user interface system is a RasberryPi with a LCDTFT touchscreen. The two systems are connected through a USB capable that allows the two to communicate serially.

User Interface System

Raspberry Pi, commanding a Thin-Film-Transistor Liquid-Crystal Display touchscreen, provides users a visual interface to execute the printing process. Raspberry Pi would handle incoming gerber files in order to convert it to gcode path files. It also communicates with Arduino over Serial to transmit gcode commands and exchange information on operation status.

Real Time Control System

We choose Arduino as our platform of development since it is the fast option to achieve a viable, albeit limited, performance within the timeline of PoE. Instead of purchasing a pre-designed Arduino shield which would limit the degree of freedom when writing our firmware. Our MVP solution is a perforated board that easily sits on top of an Arduino Uno similar to other Arduino shields. We were able to customize our electronics easily and at the same time leave room for future expansions is needed. Below is the schematic of the whole real time control system.

On the main board which sits directly on Arduino, we have three A4988 stepper motor drivers and connections to limit switches and stepper motors are also provided.

On the the first extension board sits a couple of LEDs and a quadrature encoder switch. A press down on the quadrature encoder switch allows the user to cycle through between four modes of zero: Manually(motor drivers disengaged), X, Y, and Z. The quadrature encoder ticks provides information on the distance and the direction the motor should be traveling in.

The second extension board has a power relay and a N-channel MOSFET to drive the dremel motor throughout the milling process. The power relay is separately controlled by Arduino, ensuring the safe physical disconnect from a dangerous cutting tool from power when it is not enabled.

An E-switch can also be seen on the outer housing of the gantry for users to cut off power quickly in case of emergencies.

Power

The power of our system is spilt upon between the control and the actuation. Both are connected to a 12V wall adapter. 12V for the control system is stepped down by an Arduino to 5V, which then goes onto power both Arduino and Raspberry Pi.

Thermal management is another important part of the electrical system. Due to the large amount of current that flow through the system to power the DC motor and three different stepper motors. Each stepper motor draws about 0.5A while the DC motor would pull about 1.3A. For passive heat dissipation, heat sinks are put on all three A4988 stepper motor modules while a large, through-hole N-MOSFET with a large heat conductive back is used. We also used a computer fan to circulate air actively across the electronics.
